Mapping

Comparative map of Robinson Crusoe and its inspirations

This is a map of the possible inspirations for Robinson Crusoe. There are four texts layed against Robinson Crusoe (purple). An Historical Relation of the Island Ceylon (Green), A New Voyage Round the World (Blue) A Cruising Voyage round the World (red), The Man in the Moone (yellow).

Reading the comparative map

By Strict;y considering the data in front of us (for a discussion on all the things that went wrong with the data, please see the mapping section) there are a few conclusions we can draw. First we can see that there is no perfect text that matches the trajectory of Robinson Crusoe. There are parts that line up quite nicely. For example Ceylon and Crusoe visit similar places in Western Africa, and A New voyage and Cruso visit similar places in South America. All of the texts have heavy action in Europe, and this is logical as Europe was a starting or ending point for many of the voyages, as they were colonial.

You might start by looking for overlaps: ports, islands, or regions that appear in multiple works. Then notice what is missing or displaced—places that Crusoe alludes to indirectly, or routes that are altered, simplified, or exaggerated.

The map is not meant as a definitive reconstruction of any single journey, but as a way of seeing how these texts share a common geography while also reworking it. As you move between the map and the individual collections, think about how changes in setting also reshape genre, character, and narrative emphasis.

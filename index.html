About the project

A digital humanities project tracing the journeys, islands, and imagined geographies of Robinson Crusoe.

Digital humanities & literary cartography

Why map Robinson Crusoe?

Robinson Crusoe (1719) is one of the most influential travel narratives in English literature. It draws on real-world voyage literature while imagining an island, shipwreck, and return journey that have shaped how readers picture the “desert island” for over three hundred years.

Mapping Robinson Crusoe explores how space, movement, and location work in the novel. The project plots key scenes onto interactive maps, compares them with historical travel accounts, and highlights the tensions between real geography and fictional space.

Project questions

This project asks how cartographic visualisation can change our reading of the novel:

  • Where exactly can we place Crusoe’s island on a modern map?
  • How does Defoe combine real Atlantic and South American geographies with imagined spaces?
  • What happens when we layer the novel against the travel narratives it draws on?

Methods

The project brings together close reading, historical research, and digital mapping:

  • Encoding locations and routes mentioned in the text.
  • Comparing Crusoe’s route with seventeenth- and eighteenth-century travel accounts.
  • Visualising uncertainty and ambiguity in place references.
  • Designing maps for both scholarly use and classroom teaching.

Who is this for?

The site is designed for students, teachers, and researchers interested in eighteenth-century literature, cartography, and digital humanities. It can be used:

  • In literature courses to discuss space, colonialism, and travel writing.
  • In digital humanities courses as a case study in literary mapping.
  • By researchers as a jumping-off point for further work on Defoe and geography.

As the project develops, new maps, data, and teaching materials will be added. The goal is not to fix one “true” Crusoe map, but to make visible how many different ways the novel can be placed on the globe.

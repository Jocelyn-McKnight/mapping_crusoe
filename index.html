Mapping Robinson Crusoe and Its Inspirations

The Mapping Robinson Crusoe project investigates the iconic 18th century text Robinson Crusoe and its potential sources of inspiration through mapping. We attempt to see the similarities between Crusoe and similar literatures through a comparative study on locations in text, which has been visualized with maps.

Welcome

Welcome to the Mapping Robinson Crusoe project

Robinson Crusoe has been studied countless times over the last few centuries. It is sometimes considered the first novel in the Englis language, though that is contentious. The novel was recieved positively during it times, though not without critics from such as Swift and Pope.

A debate that has risen in Crusoe studies and 18th century literary studies is that of the sources of inspiration for the novel. Though Robinson Crusoe was extremely propular, it did not in fact appear out of thin air. Travel literature and stories of castaways could be traced all the way back to the Homeric period with the Odyssey. However, Defoe was more likely drawing on some of his contemporaries. Books like The Mighty Kingdom of Krinke Kesmes by Hendrik Smeeks and stories like that of Alexander Selkirk have recieved particular attention in scholarship.

While literary evidence and close readings have brought together very compelling arguements about the origins of Robinson Crusoe, this project offers a different approach. A comparative study on the location in the travel literature before Crusoe in an attempt to find patterns and possibly inspirations. The primary goal of this project is to compare the movements in the previous travel account to see if there are any similarities. The secondary goal is to examin the broader trends in travel literature through place and space. We contend that through digital mapping and comparative GIS, we can add new evidence to the debate on the inspirations of Robinson Crusoe.
